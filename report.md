# Technical Assessment: Sentinel-2 Change Analysis Report

## 1. Method

### Change Detection Algorithms Chosen
The change detection pipeline uses a **Multi-Method Approach** consisting of three complementary change indices:
1. **Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)** differencing to assess changes in vegetative and bare-earth cover, explicitly identifying areas that transitioned from Vegetation -> Bare Ground.
2. **Visible Atmospherically Resistant Index (VARI) / Green-Red Vegetation Index (GRVI)** differencing as a secondary optical vegetative validation.
3. **Spectral Euclidean Distance (SED)** across all multi-spectral bands to capture general spectral magnitude shifts (e.g., changes in soil moisture, water bodies, or profound brightness changes).

The final binary change map is generated by computing the **intersection** of these methods. A pixel is only flagged as a "Change" if it represents a significant shift in *both* its individual vegetation/earth structural reflection (NDVI/GRVI) and its overall spectral distance (SED).

### Analytical Rationale & Dealing with Atmospheric Noise
A significant challenge in the provided dataset was the presence of **haze** in the September (Date 2) imagery. Furthermore, the base assignment did not provide a **Near-Infrared (NIR)** band, preventing the use of standard NDVI.

To overcome this, the following pipeline was implemented:
- **Band 8 Injection**: Band 8 (NIR) imagery was independently fetched, resampled, and re-projected using the custom-built **[Earth Data Extraction Tool](https://ee-srijaroy.projects.earthengine.app/view/earth-data-extraction-tool)** on Google Earth Engine to match the analysis grids. The custom injection of `B08.tif` unlocked industry-grade NDVI classification mapping.
- **Dark Channel Prior (DCP) Dehazing**: Since the input dataset had extensive scattering from haze in the visible spectrum, DCP was applied. This method models the atmospheric light and transmission map, recovering the true scene radiance for the September imagery before comparison.
- **Relative Radiometric Normalization**: To ensure comparability after dehazing, Histogram Matching was used to broadly align the brightness distributions between dates, followed by a linear refinement using Pseudo-Invariant Features (PIFs) to ensure unchanged terrain remained stable between the two scenes.
- **Dynamic Thresholding**: Otsu's optimal thresholding method was utilized to determine data-driven cutoffs for "significant change" tailored to the statistical distribution of both the GRVI variations and Spectral Distances, rather than arbitrarily hardcoding limits. Finally, Small object removal (morphological cleaning) was used to mitigate spurious, noisy pixels often generated by residual atmospheric artifacts.

## 2. Results

The processing output detected changes corresponding to approximately **3.10%** of the Area of Interest (AOI), amounting to an aggregate area of roughly **8.22 km²**.

Specifically, Terrain Segmentation classifying NDVI values highlighted that up to **15.94 km²** consisted of pixels transitioning from **Vegetation** (NDVI ≥ 0.2) to **Bare Ground** (0 ≤ NDVI < 0.2). However, by actively intersecting this raw classification with the SED and GRVI difference arrays to aggressively filter out atmospheric haze, the final high-confidence change area was reliably condensed to the 8.22 km² cited above.

* **Change Distribution**: The visualizations (e.g., `change_detection_map.html` and `change_analysis.png`) reveal clustered, highly-localized areas of extreme confidence change across the landscape, rather than uniform noise scattered universally. 
* **September Scan Gap Constraint**: It is imperative to note the presence of horizontal "merging line gaps" within the September Date 2 source imagery. This is an unavoidable data-acquisition scan spacing error from the satellite sensor array. While morphological filtering algorithms were applied to actively mask these rows and prevent massive continuous line anomalies, certain adjacent pixel disruptions resulting from the scan anomaly still bleed into the final polygon results as mathematical "changes". These should not be misconstrued as physical geographical changes on the ground.

## 3. Interpretation

What might these changes represent? Given the context that the Area of Interest represents an **open-pit mining site in Zambia**, the detected 8.22 km² of highly-confident changes most likely represent:

- **Land clearing**: The active stripping of topsoil and vegetative clearing around the periphery of the expanding pits, indicated by a significant transition of Vegetation into Bare Ground via the negative shifts in NDVI and GRVI indices.
- **Urban / Industrial expansion**: Active mining excavation, the deepening of existing pits, and the physical deposition of new earth waste or tailings. This translates dynamically to the extreme spectral divergence (SED shifts) we detect against prior soil states.
- **Vegetation change & Seasonal variation**: General phenological shifts, momentary surface moisture differences, or seasonal vegetative changes happening between the August and September windows in the surrounding undisturbed land.
- **Noise or artifacts**: Despite dynamic thresholding and dehazing, some of the smaller detected polygons or linear breaks are fundamentally tied to the "scan merging line gap" sensor artifact present within the September Date 2 source matrix. Other noise may simply be residual atmospheric inconsistencies or shifting shadows inside the highly variable topography of the pit.
